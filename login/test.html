About the cardiovascular disease

Cardiovascular disease (CVD) refers to a group of disorders affecting the heart and blood vessels, including conditions like heart disease, stroke, and hypertension. It often results from risk factors such as poor diet, lack of exercise, smoking, and high blood pressure. Early prevention and treatment are crucial for managing CVD

About the diabetic

Diabetes is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by high levels of blood glucose (sugar) due to the body’s inability to produce or effectively use insulin. Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas that helps regulate blood sugar. There are two main types: Type 1 diabetes, which is an autoimmune condition where the body attacks insulin-producing cells, and Type 2 diabetes, which occurs when the body becomes resistant to insulin or doesn't produce enough. Type 2 is more common and often linked to lifestyle factors like obesity, physical inactivity, and poor diet. Symptoms of diabetes include frequent urination, excessive thirst, fatigue, blurred vision, and slow healing of wounds. If not managed properly, it can lead to serious complications such as heart disease, kidney failure, nerve damage, and vision loss. Diagnosis is done through blood tests like fasting blood glucose, HbA1c, and oral glucose tolerance tests. Management involves lifestyle modifications, regular monitoring of blood sugar, medication, and sometimes insulin therapy. Early detection and consistent management are crucial in preventing complications. Education, dietary control, physical activity, and medication adherence play vital roles in improving quality of life for people living with diabetes.

About the Hypertension

Hypertension, commonly known as high blood pressure, is a chronic medical condition where the force of the blood against the artery walls is consistently too high. It is often termed the "silent killer" because it typically presents no symptoms until significant damage has occurred to the heart, arteries, kidneys, or other organs. Blood pressure is measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg) and recorded as two values: systolic (the pressure when the heart beats) and diastolic (the pressure when the heart rests between beats). A reading of 140/90 mmHg or higher is generally considered hypertensive. Hypertension is classified as either primary (essential), which has no identifiable cause and tends to develop gradually over years, or secondary, which is caused by underlying conditions such as kidney disease or hormonal disorders. Risk factors include a sedentary lifestyle, poor diet (especially high salt intake), obesity, smoking, stress, excessive alcohol consumption, and a family history of hypertension. Left untreated, hypertension can lead to heart attacks, strokes, kidney failure, and vision loss. Management includes lifestyle modifications like regular physical activity, a heart-healthy diet, weight management, and, if necessary, medication. Early detection and consistent monitoring are essential for preventing long-term complications and improving quality of life.
